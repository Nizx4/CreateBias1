Bias Library

Explore the most common cognitive biases, each explained simply with real-world examples and practical tips.

Confirmation Bias

What it is: The tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information that confirms your existing beliefs.
Example: Only reading news that supports your political views while ignoring opposing perspectives.
Why it happens: Our brains want to avoid discomfort from conflicting information, so we seek out what feels familiar or "right."
How to reduce it: Actively seek out different viewpoints and challenge your own assumptions.

Anchoring Bias

What it is: Relying too heavily on the first piece of information (the "anchor") when making decisions.
Example: If the first price you see for a phone is $1,000, all later prices seem cheap by comparison—even if they're still expensive.
Why it happens: The brain uses initial information as a mental shortcut, making it hard to adjust away from it.
How to reduce it: Be aware of anchors and consider information from multiple sources before deciding.

Availability Heuristic

What it is: Overestimating the importance or frequency of events that come to mind easily.
Example: After seeing news about airplane crashes, you might think flying is more dangerous than it really is.
Why it happens: Events that are vivid or recent are easier to recall, making them seem more common.
How to reduce it: Look for actual statistics and evidence, not just what’s memorable.

Dunning-Kruger Effect

What it is: People with low ability at something often overestimate their skill, while experts may underestimate theirs.
Example: A beginner at chess thinking they could beat a grandmaster after learning the rules.
Why it happens: Lack of knowledge makes it hard to recognize one’s own mistakes or gaps.
How to reduce it: Stay humble, seek feedback, and keep learning—no matter your skill level.

Bandwagon Effect

What it is: The tendency to do or believe things because many other people do.
Example: Liking a song just because it’s popular, not because you actually enjoy it.
Why it happens: Social acceptance feels good, and we’re wired to fit in with groups.
How to reduce it: Pause and ask yourself if you truly agree or just want to belong.

Loss Aversion

What it is: The tendency to prefer avoiding losses over acquiring similar gains.
Example: Feeling more upset about losing $20 than happy about finding $20.
Why it happens: Losses trigger stronger emotional reactions than gains of the same size.
How to reduce it: Focus on the bigger picture and long-term outcomes, not just avoiding losses.

Negativity Bias

What it is: Paying more attention to negative experiences than positive ones.
Example: Remembering one criticism for years but quickly forgetting compliments.
Why it happens: Our brains evolved to notice threats for survival, so negatives stand out.
How to reduce it: Practice gratitude and intentionally focus on positive events.

Hindsight Bias

What it is: Believing, after something happens, that you "knew it all along."
Example: After a sports game, claiming you always knew who would win.
Why it happens: Our minds reconstruct memories to make events seem more predictable.
How to reduce it: Write down your predictions before events to check your accuracy later.

Self-Serving Bias

What it is: Attributing successes to yourself but blaming failures on outside factors.
Example: Taking credit for good grades but blaming bad ones on unfair teachers.
Why it happens: Protecting self-esteem feels good, so we unconsciously shift blame.
How to reduce it: Reflect honestly on your actions and accept responsibility for mistakes.

Sunk Cost Fallacy

What it is: Continuing something just because you’ve already invested time, money, or effort—even if it’s not working.
Example: Finishing a boring movie because you already watched an hour of it.
Why it happens: We want our past investments to “count,” so we avoid feeling wasteful.
How to reduce it: Focus on future benefits, not past costs, when making decisions.
